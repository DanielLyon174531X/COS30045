Health Expenditure (% of GDP)

This section explores how much each country invests in hospital care as a percentage of their Gross Domestic Product (GDP), based on OECD data from 2016.

Hospital Expenditure (% GDP) by Country - 2016

Figure 1. Hospital expenditure (% of GDP) by country, 2016

Source: OECD Data Explorer – Hospital Expenditure (2016)

Analysis of Hospital Expenditure by Country (2016)

This bar chart visualizes the percentage of GDP that 34 countries allocated to hospital services in 2016. Denmark leads the group at 4.689%, followed closely by Japan, Greece, and Australia—all exceeding 3.6%. At the other end of the scale, countries such as the United Kingdom (1.162%), Luxembourg (1.327%), and Sweden (1.342%) allocated relatively little to hospital expenditure.

From a public health policy perspective, this distribution raises important questions about the prioritisation of healthcare within national budgets. While GDP percentages do not capture the full complexity of a nation’s health system, they do reflect government and system-level commitment to hospital infrastructure and services—a critical pillar of public health capacity.

Notably, several high-income countries with advanced healthcare systems (e.g. Germany, France, Canada) still fall below 3.5% of GDP in hospital spending. This suggests that even among OECD nations, investment in hospital services varies considerably despite similar economic capacities.

For researchers and policymakers advocating for increased public health funding, this visualisation serves as a compelling starting point. It highlights significant disparities and invites deeper exploration of the relationship between hospital spending and health outcomes (e.g. life expectancy, avoidable mortality, or patient satisfaction). As additional datasets are incorporated into this project—such as perceived health status, healthcare access equity, and preventative care spending—we aim to show that nations investing more consistently in health tend to achieve better public health outcomes.

Hospital Expenditure (% GDP) by Country - 2021

Figure 2. Hospital expenditure (% of GDP) by country, 2021

Source: OECD Data Explorer – Hospital Expenditure

Analysis of Hospital Expenditure by Country (2021)

This chart shows the hospital expenditure as a percentage of GDP across countries in 2021. Compared to 2016, most nations experienced an increase in hospital spending—likely influenced by the global COVID-19 pandemic, which placed unprecedented pressure on healthcare systems. Notably, countries such as Portugal (from 3.545% to 4.246%), Cyprus (2.338% to 4.448%), and Spain (3.432% to 4.213%) saw significant increases, indicating a ramp-up in public health investments.

Some high-income nations also increased spending: France (3.49% → 3.695%), Germany (2.866% → 3.114%), and Australia (3.648% → 3.726%). However, the rate of increase varied. For instance, Canada slightly decreased its hospital spending (2.577% → 2.437%), possibly due to shifts in how care was delivered (e.g. more outpatient or community-based services).

Despite economic growth, some countries like Sweden (1.342% → 1.309%) and the UK (1.162% → 1.412%) remained relatively low in terms of hospital-related spending as a share of GDP. These patterns may reflect ongoing efficiency strategies, different health system models, or evolving policy priorities.

The comparison suggests that external shocks like pandemics can significantly reshape national health spending. It also highlights how different countries adapt their investment strategies in hospital care, which may have long-term implications for health system resilience, preparedness, and population outcomes.

Hospital Expenditure Trends by Country (2016–2023)

Figure 3. Line chart of hospital expenditure as % of GDP by country, 2016–2023

Source: OECD Health Statistics – Hospital Expenditure (2016–2023)

Analysis of Hospital Expenditure Trends by Country (2016–2023)

This interactive line chart illustrates trends in hospital expenditure as a percentage of GDP across OECD countries from 2016 to 2023. While most countries show a notable increase in 2020 and 2021, likely reflecting the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, the trends vary significantly across nations.

For example, Denmark, Cyprus, and Portugal consistently allocate more than 4% of GDP to hospital services in recent years, indicating sustained investment in hospital infrastructure. In contrast, countries such as Israel, Luxembourg, and the United Kingdom maintain relatively low hospital expenditure, generally under 1.6% of GDP.

It is important to note that not all countries have reported data through to 2023. While most countries provided data up to 2022, only a limited number have published figures for 2023 at the time of writing. As such, comparisons beyond 2021 should be made with caution.

These spending patterns highlight how healthcare priorities differ internationally and prompt further questions about the relationship between expenditure levels and public health outcomes, particularly in times of crisis. The chart enables users to explore individual country trajectories over time, offering insights into both long-term strategies and short-term responses in national healthcare investment.

Average Hospital Expenditure Trends Across Countries (2016–2023)

Figure 4. Average hospital expenditure (% of GDP) across countries by year

Source: OECD Health Statistics – Hospital Expenditure

This chart displays the average annual hospital expenditure as a percentage of GDP across all reporting OECD countries from 2016 to 2023. It highlights a distinct increase in spending during 2020 and 2021, which coincides with the global COVID-19 pandemic. The average rose sharply in 2020 as many countries boosted health budgets in response to public health emergencies, then remained elevated through 2021. In 2022 and 2023, the average begins to level out or slightly decline, though this should be interpreted with caution—data availability drops significantly in the final two years, with most countries reporting only up to 2022 and relatively few providing figures for 2023. Despite this limitation, the trend provides useful context for understanding how health expenditure evolved globally during and after the pandemic years.
