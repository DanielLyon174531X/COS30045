Analysis of Perceived Health Status Over Time

This line chart tracks the percentage of people in selected countries who report being in “good” or “very good” health, broken down by income quintile (Q1 = lowest 20%, Q5 = highest 20%) over multiple years.

A clear trend is visible across almost all countries: perceived health status dropped noticeably during the COVID-19 pandemic years (2020–2022). This decline affected both lower- and higher-income groups, but often more sharply among those in the lowest income quintile. The data highlights how health crises can widen socio-economic disparities in well-being, even in high-income nations.

One particularly striking case is Japan, where perceived health status is significantly lower than in other countries — for both low- and high-income groups. Even among Japan’s wealthiest 20%, fewer than 45% report being in good or very good health in recent years. In contrast, high-income groups in other countries typically report rates between 70% and 95%. This may reflect Japan’s much older population, as older individuals may be more likely to report lower perceived health, even when objective health indicators like life expectancy remain high.

These patterns suggest that income and age demographics both strongly influence how people perceive their own health — and that public health investments should consider not just clinical outcomes, but also how people feel about their health, especially during periods of crisis.
