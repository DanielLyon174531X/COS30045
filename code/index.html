OECD Health Expenditure Data Exploration

This website presents interactive data visualisations based on OECD Health Statistics. The project aims to explore how different countries allocate public resources to healthcare, particularly hospital expenditure as a percentage of GDP. By examining these patterns alongside other health indicators in future charts, we seek to highlight the importance of sustained investment in public health systems for better outcomes and equity.

Analysis of Hospital Expenditure by Country (2016)

This bar chart visualizes the percentage of GDP that 34 countries allocated to hospital services in 2016. Denmark leads the group at 4.689%, followed closely by Japan, Greece, and Australia—all exceeding 3.6%. At the other end of the scale, countries such as the United Kingdom (1.162%), Luxembourg (1.327%), and Sweden (1.342%) allocated relatively little to hospital expenditure.

From a public health policy perspective, this distribution raises important questions about the prioritisation of healthcare within national budgets. While GDP percentages do not capture the full complexity of a nation’s health system, they do reflect government and system-level commitment to hospital infrastructure and services—a critical pillar of public health capacity.

Notably, several high-income countries with advanced healthcare systems (e.g. Germany, France, Canada) still fall below 3.5% of GDP in hospital spending. This suggests that even among OECD nations, investment in hospital services varies considerably despite similar economic capacities.

For researchers and policymakers advocating for increased public health funding, this visualisation serves as a compelling starting point. It highlights significant disparities and invites deeper exploration of the relationship between hospital spending and health outcomes (e.g. life expectancy, avoidable mortality, or patient satisfaction). As additional datasets are incorporated into this project—such as perceived health status, healthcare access equity, and preventative care spending—we aim to show that nations investing more consistently in health tend to achieve better public health outcomes.

Perceived Health Status Over Time

Source: OECD Data Explorer – Perceived Health Status (2016–2023)

Analysis of Perceived Health Status Over Time

This line chart tracks the percentage of people in selected countries who report being in “good” or “very good” health, broken down by income quintile (Q1 = lowest 20%, Q5 = highest 20%) over multiple years.

A clear trend is visible across almost all countries: perceived health status dropped noticeably during the COVID-19 pandemic years (2020–2022). This decline affected both lower- and higher-income groups, but often more sharply among those in the lowest income quintile. The data highlights how health crises can widen socio-economic disparities in well-being, even in high-income nations.

One particularly striking case is Japan, where perceived health status is significantly lower than in other countries — for both low- and high-income groups. Even among Japan’s wealthiest 20%, fewer than 45% report being in good or very good health in recent years. In contrast, high-income groups in other countries typically report rates between 70% and 95%. This may reflect Japan’s much older population, as older individuals may be more likely to report lower perceived health, even when objective health indicators like life expectancy remain high.

These patterns suggest that income and age demographics both strongly influence how people perceive their own health — and that public health investments should consider not just clinical outcomes, but also how people feel about their health, especially during periods of crisis.

Spending vs Health Gap

Comparing the two charts—hospital expenditure as a percentage of GDP and perceived health status by income group—reveals notable trends and disparities.

For instance, Sweden spent just 1.34% of GDP on hospitals in 2016, significantly lower than Norway’s 3.54%. Correspondingly, perceived health among Sweden’s lowest income group (Q1) was 63.6%, compared to 69% in Norway—suggesting that higher hospital investment may correlate with better perceived health among disadvantaged groups.

Interestingly, Denmark had the highest hospital expenditure at 4.69% of GDP, yet its Q1 perceived health score was only 66.0%. This might reflect higher national health awareness or investment in response to existing health burdens, rather than purely resulting in better outcomes.

A stark contrast is visible in the United Kingdom, where hospital expenditure was just 1.16% of GDP, and Q1 perceived health was relatively low at 59.6%. On the other hand, Australia spent 3.65% of GDP and saw a much higher Q1 perceived health score of 84.1%.

One notable outlier is Japan, which had the second-highest hospital spending at 3.72% of GDP, yet reported the lowest perceived health status: only 28% in Q1 and 39.4% even in the wealthiest quintile (Q5). This may be influenced by Japan’s aging population, which likely impacts self-reported health perceptions.

Overall, while higher spending on hospital services appears to support better perceived health—especially for lower-income groups—exceptions like Japan and Denmark suggest that other demographic or cultural factors (e.g. aging, awareness, expectations, or perhaps even climate) also shape how health is perceived.

Data note: All Q1 perceived health values referenced are from 2016, except for Australia, which uses 2017 data due to lack of availability in 2016.

About This Prototype

This project represents a working prototype built to practice the complete process of data-driven visualisation—from data collection and cleaning to JSON transformation and D3.js implementation. Rather than aiming for a final polished product at this stage, the current version serves as a testbed to trial ideas, refine our workflows, and experiment with interactivity and formatting.

Key features implemented in this prototype include:

  • Animated bar chart for hospital expenditure (% of GDP), with each bar growing on page load.
  • Tooltip on hover for both bar and line charts, providing precise values.
  • Country dropdown menu for the line chart, allowing user-driven comparison across nations.
  • Clean code structure with functions split by responsibility (e.g., axes rendering, line drawing, tooltips), following clean code principles.
  • Consistent styling and layout across charts, using reusable margins and scales.
  • Data preprocessing pipeline using Python to extract and structure raw OECD data into clean JSON.

The final version of this project will include additional visualisations, more nuanced comparisons and an integrated narrative that clearly argues the case for increased health investment using both data and storytelling.

For now, this prototype demonstrates a working foundation—both technically and analytically—for what will become a more complex and polished interactive report.
